# AI Detection Analysis: Suncor Equity Research Report

## Test Methodology

Tested the Investment Thesis + Executive Summary passage (~300 words) from README.md against two AI detection tools, before and after applying the writing skill rewrite.

**Tools used:**
- **GPTZero** (gptzero.me) — Model 4.3b, claims 99% accuracy
- **ZeroGPT** (zerogpt.com) — free, provides AI percentage score

**Passage tested:** Investment Thesis + Executive Summary (the most visible, highest-traffic section of the report)

## Results Summary

| Tool | Original Score | Rewritten Score | Change | Original Verdict | Rewritten Verdict |
|------|---------------|----------------|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **GPTZero** | AI 90% / Mixed 10% / Human 0% | AI 100% / Mixed 0% / Human 0% | +10% AI (worse) | "Highly confident AI generated" | "Highly confident AI generated" |
| **ZeroGPT** | 54.2% AI GPT | 47.4% AI GPT | -6.8% AI (better) | "Likely generated by AI/GPT" | "Likely Human written, may include AI parts" |

## Key Finding: The Tools Disagree

The two detectors gave **opposite results** from the same rewrite. GPTZero scored the rewrite worse; ZeroGPT scored it better. This confirms what the research literature says: AI detection is unreliable and inconsistent, especially for formal/technical writing.

## Why the Original Scores as AI

Both tools flagged the same types of sentences in the original:

### 1. Template Repetition (Primary trigger)
The original's Executive Summary uses the same template 6 times in one paragraph:
- "FY2025 upstream production **reached a record** 860,000 bbls/d"
- "Q4 2025 **setting an all-time quarterly record** of 909,000 bbls/d"
- "throughput **reached a record** 480,000 bbls/d"
- "refined product sales **set a record** at 623,000 bbls/d"
- "Refinery utilization **hit** 103%"
- "Net debt **was reduced** 54%"

This `[metric] [verb] [number]` template repeating 6 times in a row is exactly what LLMs produce. Low burstiness, low perplexity.

### 2. Uniform Sentence Length
The original has 3 consecutive sentences averaging 35+ words each in the Executive Summary. No short sentences. No rhythm variation. AI detectors measure the coefficient of variation in sentence length; uniform = AI.

### 3. Formal Conjunctions
"However" and "Despite these achievements" are statistically overrepresented in AI text (4.6x and 3.2x respectively, per GPTZero research). The original uses "However" as the sole conjunction between the bull/bear framing.

### 4. Zero Contractions
Not a single contraction in 300 words. Human analytical writing naturally includes "doesn't," "it's," "won't." Zero contractions is a strong AI signal.

### 5. Passive/Impersonal Voice
"Net debt was reduced," "the recommendation is to hold." Passive constructions without an agent are more common in AI output.

## Why GPTZero Scored the Rewrite *Worse*

This is the most interesting finding. The rewrite followed every writing skill rule (contractions, short sentences, active voice, human conjunctions), yet GPTZero gave it a higher AI score (100% vs 90%).

**Hypothesis: GPTZero's Model 4.3b has learned to detect "humanized AI" patterns.**

Modern AI detectors are trained on a corpus that includes both raw AI output AND AI output that has been edited to sound more human. The specific techniques in the writing skill (adding "But" instead of "However," inserting short punchy sentences, using contractions) are the same techniques commonly recommended in "how to avoid AI detection" guides. GPTZero's latest model appears to have adapted to flag these exact patterns.

Evidence:
- GPTZero flagged "But the reported financials tell a different story" as one of the "most AI sentences" in the rewrite. The "But [contrast]" pattern, while more human than "However," is now itself a recognized pattern in humanized-AI text.
- Short declarative sentences like "None of that was commodity-driven." and "Hold existing positions." were flagged, suggesting the detector treats abrupt brevity in financial text as suspicious.

## Why ZeroGPT Scored the Rewrite *Better*

ZeroGPT uses a different detection model that appears less sophisticated at detecting humanization. The traditional signals worked:
- Contractions ("doesn't," "it's") moved the text toward human
- Sentence length variety (7-word and 40-word sentences mixed) increased burstiness
- The opening paragraph's conversational rhythm passed as human

The classification verdict flipped from "Likely AI" to "Likely Human" even though the score only dropped ~7 points. This shows that small stylistic changes can push text across decision boundaries in simpler detectors.

## The Fundamental Problem: Financial Analysis Text Is Inherently "AI-Like"

The deeper issue is that **equity research writing is structurally similar to AI output regardless of who writes it.** Both human analysts and LLMs:
- List metrics in structured sequences
- Use formal, precise language
- Present data-heavy sentences with similar templates
- Follow predictable paragraph structures (thesis, evidence, conclusion)

A human equity analyst at Goldman Sachs would write sentences that look almost identical to what an LLM produces: "Revenue grew 8% to $4.2B, beating consensus of $4.0B." This sentence is factual, template-driven, and predictable. It would flag as AI on any detector.

**The detectors aren't wrong about the style. They're wrong about the author.** Financial analysis is a domain where human and AI writing naturally converge, making detection fundamentally unreliable.

## What the Writing Skill Actually Improves

Even though AI detectors are unreliable for this text type, the rewrite is genuinely better writing:

| Dimension | Before | After | Impact |
|-----------|--------|-------|--------|
| Kill-list words | 10+ "However," 8+ "significant," etc. | Zero in prose | Cleaner, more confident prose |
| Contractions | 0 across 135KB | 56 across all files | Natural, human rhythm |
| Sentence variety | Uniform 30-40 word sentences | Mix of 7-word punches and 40-word compounds | Readable, engaging |
| Point-first | ~50% of paragraphs | ~95% of paragraphs | Reader gets the headline immediately |
| "So what" | Many naked statistics | Stats with context | Reader understands why they should care |
| Verb intensity | Generic "grew/fell/declined" | Matched to magnitude ("plunged" for 54%, "dipped" for 8%) | Precise, vivid |
| "Record" repetition | 157 instances of "record" | Rotated with "all-time high," "best-ever," "topped" | Less monotonous |

## Recommendations

1. **Don't use AI detectors as a quality gate for financial writing.** They're unreliable for this domain. A 54% AI score on the original and a 100% AI score on the clearly-improved rewrite proves the tools can't distinguish quality analytical prose from AI output.

2. **The writing skill improves readability, not detectability.** Apply it because the prose reads better, not to fool detectors. The WSJ/Bloomberg target voice is worth pursuing for its own sake.

3. **If detection avoidance matters, the only reliable approach is:**
   - Include genuinely personal observations or opinions that only the author could have
   - Reference specific conversations, site visits, or firsthand experiences
   - Use domain jargon that's idiosyncratic to the analyst's coverage universe
   - These are content-level signals, not style-level signals

4. **GPTZero has likely trained on "humanized AI" patterns.** The writing skill's techniques (contractions, short sentences, "But" instead of "However") are now recognized as humanization markers. Style-level changes alone won't fool modern detectors.
