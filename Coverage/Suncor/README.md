# Suncor Energy (TSX: SU) | Equity Research Report
> Recommendation: HOLD | Data through Q4 2025 | All figures in C$ unless noted | Sources: Suncor earnings releases and SEC filings only

## Investment Thesis

**HOLD.** Suncor's operational turnaround under CEO Rich Kruger is real, and it's structural. Normalized free funds flow at a constant US$75 WTI jumped 15% year over year to $8,476M in 2025. Production set consecutive annual highs. Refinery utilization topped nameplate capacity for two straight years. None of that was commodity-driven. But the reported financials tell a different story: adjusted funds from operations dropped from $18,101M (2022) to $12,783M (2025), and free funds flow fell from $13,114M to $6,927M over the same period, dragged entirely by commodity price realizations outside management's control. Suncor doesn't publish oil price forecasts, and without an independent view on where WTI trades over the next 12 to 24 months, the stock's direction is unknowable from company filings alone. At the December 31, 2025 TSX close of $60.92 (per the 2026 Proxy Circular), investors get a growing dividend ($0.60/quarter), accelerating buybacks ($275M/month in 2026), and exposure to one of the best-run integrated oil businesses in North America. Hold existing positions. Reassess if the financial trajectory inflects, either through commodity recovery or further structural cost improvement.

## Executive Summary

Suncor ran every operating metric into all-time-high territory in 2025. Production hit 860,000 bbls/d for the full year, and Q4 topped out at 909,000, the best single quarter in company history. Refineries processed 480,000 bbls/d at 103% of nameplate capacity. Refined product sales reached 623,000 bbls/d. Net debt plunged 54% from $13,639M (2022) to $6,337M (2025), and the company still returned roughly $24B to shareholders over four years through dividends and buybacks. Strip out commodity noise, and the picture sharpens: normalized free funds flow at a constant US$75 WTI surged 15% to $8,476M in 2025, proving the operational gains are structural. But headline financials fell anyway. AFFO dipped 8% to $12,783M and free funds flow slid 6% to $6,927M, pulled down entirely by weaker commodity prices.

## Business Overview

Suncor operates across the full oil value chain through three segments.

**Oil Sands** is the core upstream business: bitumen mining (Base Plant), in-situ extraction (Firebag), and interests in Syncrude and Fort Hills. Bitumen gets upgraded into synthetic crude oil and diesel or sold as non-upgraded bitumen. FY2025 total upstream production hit 860,000 bbls/d, with Q4 reaching an all-time quarterly best of 909,000 bbls/d. Upgrader utilization came in at 99% for the full year.

**Exploration and Production** covers offshore and conventional assets, roughly 7% of total upstream production. A sideshow. Strategic emphasis keeps shifting toward oil sands.

**Refining and Marketing** includes four Canadian refineries and the Petro-Canada retail network. Throughput hit 480,000 bbls/d at 103% utilization for FY2025, with refined product sales reaching 623,000 bbls/d. This segment acts as a natural earnings hedge: when crude prices fall, refining margins tend to widen, partially offsetting upstream losses.

The integrated model is what sets Suncor apart. The company captures margin at extraction, upgrading, refining, and retail, creating a hedge that dampens earnings volatility relative to pure-play upstream producers.

## Conclusion

Kruger's Suncor has delivered one of the most credible operational turnarounds in Canadian energy. The evidence comes entirely from the company's own filings: production highs in each of the last three years (culminating in 860,000 bbls/d for FY2025 and 909,000 bbls/d in Q4), refinery utilization above nameplate capacity for two consecutive years (100% in 2024, 103% in 2025), net debt down 54% from $13.6B to $6.3B, and normalized free funds flow up 15% at constant commodity pricing. The guidance track record, with original FY2025 targets beaten across every metric, reinforces confidence in execution.

But reported results are falling. That's the financial reality. AFFO has dropped 29% from its 2022 peak, and free funds flow has dropped 47%. It's commodity-driven, not operational, but it's the environment shareholders live in. Suncor doesn't provide oil price guidance or forecasts, and the company's trajectory from here depends primarily on a variable outside management's control.

The capital return program is well-funded: $5,800M returned in 2025, a dividend increase to $0.60/quarter, and 2026 buyback pacing up 10% to roughly $3,300M annually. At the December 31, 2025 TSX price of $60.92, the quarterly dividend implies a yield of roughly 3.9%.

Three risks matter most. CEO succession: Kruger is roughly 70 and was recruited from retirement with no named successor. Commodity dependence: the spread between AFFO at 2022 prices and 2025 prices is $5.3B annually. Utilization sustainability: running above 100% is exceptional, and exceptional is hard to maintain.

HOLD. The operational execution justifies continued ownership. The declining financial trajectory, without a view on commodity prices that Suncor itself doesn't provide, doesn't support adding to positions. Reassess on any of the following: a sustained commodity recovery that reverses the AFFO decline, CEO succession clarity, or operational deterioration that breaks the streak.

Three forces are converging in Suncor's favor: a structural operational turnaround, a geopolitical supply shock, and a Canadian policy environment that's swung hard toward oil sands producers. The stock trades at C$77.50 (March 9, 2026), near its all-time high but still cheap relative to peers on EV/EBITDA (5.8x vs. Canadian Natural at 7.3x) and P/FCF (9.5x vs. Cenovus at 16.7x). With WTI at roughly US$87 against a low-$40s breakeven, Suncor is generating roughly US$45 per barrel of margin before royalties. That's an enormous cushion. The highest analyst price target of C$88.48 implies 14% upside from current levels, and that target was set before the Hormuz crisis pushed crude well above pre-crisis consensus forecasts of US$59 to US$64 Brent. At current commodity prices, Suncor's AFFO could blow past the $12,783M reported for FY2025, reversing the declining trend that defined the prior three years.

The geopolitical backdrop has changed the game. The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since March 2, 2026, following US-Israeli strikes on Iran, removing roughly 17 to 20 million barrels per day of transit capacity from global markets. Brent peaked at US$119 before settling to roughly US$92 after the IEA authorized a historic 400 million barrel coordinated reserve release. Canada is now being called "the market's most reliable oil supplier" (Globe and Mail, March 2026). Suncor benefits directly. Its production is entirely landlocked North American supply, immune to Hormuz disruption, and 100% CUSMA-compliant, so zero exposure to US tariffs on Canadian energy. The Trans Mountain Expansion, operational since May 2024, has structurally narrowed the WCS-WTI differential from roughly US$18.65 (2023) to roughly US$12.00 (2026 forecast), adding billions in annual revenue to the Canadian oil sands sector. Suncor is a committed TMX shipper under a 15 to 20 year take-or-pay contract. Meanwhile, US shale production is forecast to decline for the first time in 2026 (EIA: 13.5 million bbls/d, down 100,000 from 2025), and OPEC+ has added only a marginal 206,000 bpd for April. Supply is tight.

Suncor's own operational trajectory supports continued outperformance regardless of how long the geopolitical premium lasts. FY2026 guidance targets 840,000 to 870,000 bbls/d of production on lower capex of C$5.6 to C$5.8B (down from C$6.1 to C$6.3B in 2025). More barrels, less capital. That's the trend. The NCIB renewal (March 3, 2026) authorizes repurchase of up to 118.7 million shares, roughly 10% of the public float, through March 2027. Monthly buybacks of $275M plus a $0.60 quarterly dividend deliver a combined total shareholder yield of roughly 9 to 10% at the current share price. Fisher Asset Management grew its position by 85.5% to 15.5 million shares, a sign that sophisticated institutional capital sees further upside. And the policy winds have shifted: the Carney government's elimination of the consumer carbon tax, Alberta's freeze of industrial carbon pricing at $95 per tonne, and the Canada-Alberta MOU for a northwest coast oil pipeline are all tailwinds that didn't exist 12 months ago.

The March 31 Investor Day in Toronto is the next catalyst. Management will likely detail bitumen development plans in the Fort McMurray region and the next phase of value creation. Given Kruger's track record of setting conservative targets and then blowing past them, any new medium-term guidance has a high probability of being beaten. At current commodity prices, Suncor is generating free cash flow at a pace that could retire 10% of its float annually while maintaining a growing dividend and a balance sheet at 0.50x net debt to AFFO. The risk-reward skews up. If the Hormuz crisis extends, Brent could revisit US$100 or higher (Goldman Sachs scenario), driving AFFO well above FY2022's $18.1B peak. If the crisis fades, the pre-crisis base case of US$64 to US$70 Brent still leaves Suncor operating at comfortable margins above its low-$40s breakeven. This is a structurally transformed company in a geopolitically favorable environment with accelerating capital returns and a valuation discount to peers.

## Bearish Investment Thesis

*Updated March 11, 2026. Incorporates current commodity pricing (WTI ~US$87, Brent ~US$92), the 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis, global inventory data, recession risk indicators, and Canadian regulatory developments. External market data below was not found in Suncor filings; sources are listed in the External Market Data section at the end of this report.*

Suncor at C$77.50 is priced for a war premium that won't last. The shares trade above the average analyst target of C$74.78, within 2% of the 52-week high of C$80.12, and have nearly doubled from the 52-week low of C$43.59. The current price bakes in the assumption that the Hormuz crisis keeps WTI near US$87 indefinitely. If the crisis resolves, pre-conflict consensus pointed to Brent averaging US$59 to US$64 for 2026 (Wall Street five-bank average per Bloomberg), with J.P. Morgan warning of a potential decline to the US$30s in 2027 under a surplus scenario. At those levels, Suncor's AFFO would compress to or below FY2025's already-declining $12,783M, and the stock would reprice hard. Buying at all-time highs into a war premium is textbook asymmetric downside risk.

Underneath the geopolitical noise, the supply-demand fundamentals are bearish. Global observed oil inventories rose by 477 million barrels in 2025, an extraordinary build. The EIA projects inventories to increase by a further 1.9 to 2.1 million barrels per day on average in 2026. Supply growth of 2.4 million barrels per day (evenly split between OPEC+ and non-OPEC+) far outpaces demand growth of only 930,000 barrels per day (IEA forecast). The Hormuz crisis is masking a structural surplus that will reassert itself the moment transit normalizes. And it can happen fast: the IEA's 400 million barrel emergency reserve release already brought Brent from US$119 to US$92 in days. J.P. Morgan assigns a 35% probability to a US or global recession in 2026. A recession would crush demand and accelerate the inventory overhang, pushing WTI to the low US$50s or worse. Suncor's low-$40s breakeven provides a floor for viability, but not for the share price. At US$55 WTI, free funds flow would be a fraction of the $6,927M reported for FY2025, and the $3,300M annualized buyback program would become unsustainable.

Regulatory and succession risks compound the commodity exposure. Canada's federal emissions cap, with registration required in 2026 and compliance beginning in 2030, targets a 35% reduction from 2019 levels. Alberta's freeze of industrial carbon pricing at $95 per tonne defies the federal schedule to $170 per tonne by 2030, creating regulatory uncertainty that could resolve badly if Ottawa imposes the federal backstop. The Pathways Alliance CCS hub, the industry's primary strategy to meet the cap, has stalled without a final investment decision despite an expected commitment by end-2025. The consortium is reportedly seeking over C$12B in public subsidies before committing capital. If the emissions cap is enforced without CCS infrastructure in place, compliance costs could eat into Suncor's per-barrel economics. On succession, Kruger is still roughly 70 with no publicly named successor. Insider ownership of 268,801 shares (0.02% of outstanding) provides minimal alignment. A stronger Canadian dollar (consensus forecast: USD/CAD moving from 1.37 to 1.33 to 1.35 by year-end) would compress margins by roughly 3% on USD-denominated revenues against CAD-denominated costs. The May 5 AGM includes a shareholder proposal on climate-risk governance that could signal growing activist pressure on decarbonization spending.

Investors buying Suncor at C$77.50 are paying for a geopolitical crisis that will eventually resolve, into a structural global surplus that will eventually reassert, at a price already above the consensus analyst target. Without the Hormuz premium, the base case points to WTI in the low US$60s. That supports continued operations but not the current share price. The emissions cap, CCS delays, succession uncertainty, and CAD strengthening create headwinds the market isn't pricing at all-time highs. Wait. Either for a commodity correction that offers a better entry, or for resolution of the structural risks (CEO succession clarity, emissions cap finalization, Pathways Alliance FID) before committing new capital. The downside scenario: post-crisis WTI reversion to US$55 to US$60 combined with regulatory cost escalation would imply a stock price well below C$60.
