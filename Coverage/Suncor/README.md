# Suncor Energy (TSX: SU) | Equity Research Report
> Recommendation: HOLD | Data through Q4 2025 | All figures in C$ unless noted | Sources: Suncor earnings releases and SEC filings only

## Investment Thesis

**HOLD.** I keep going back and forth on this one. The operating side of Suncor hasn't looked this good in the company's history, maybe ever. Normalized free funds flow at US$75 WTI came in at $8,476M for 2025, which was 15% above the prior year on the same benchmark price. That specific number cuts through the noise because it takes commodity pricing completely out of the equation. Production, refinery uptime, unit costs, all of it trending the right way, and not for a quarter or two but going on three years straight now.

The frustrating part is that none of it shows up in the headline financials. AFFO went from $18,101M in 2022 down to $12,783M, free funds flow from $13,114M to $6,927M. Oil prices did that, not the management team, but try explaining that distinction to a generalist portfolio manager scanning the income statement. Suncor won't give you a price forecast or disclose hedging, which makes it basically impossible to model forward earnings from filings alone. At $60.92 on the TSX as of year-end (that's from the 2026 Proxy Circular), you've got a $0.60 quarterly dividend, buybacks running at $275M a month in 2026, and an operating machine that I think is genuinely best-in-class for Canadian energy. Enough to justify sitting on existing shares. I wouldn't be adding here though.

## Executive Summary

I already mentioned the $8,476M normalized FFFlow number above. Worth repeating why it matters: hold WTI flat at US$75 and Suncor squeezed out $1.1 billion more free cash than the year before. That kind of year-over-year pickup doesn't come from commodity luck. Something changed in how these assets are being run.

The snapshot table below has the full operating picture, but the thing that really caught my eye was everything peaking at once. Production at 860,000 bbls/d, utilization at 103%, throughput at 480,000. Usually when you see one metric at an all-time high the others are lagging. Not here. My honest concern is whether 103% utilization is actually repeatable or if we're looking at an unusually clean operating stretch that flatters the trailing numbers. The Q2 upgrader outage, 91 days of downtime on Upgrader 1, gave us a preview of what happens when things go wrong: AFFO cratered to $2,689M that quarter.

Net debt is down 54%, from $13,639M to $6,337M, while the company was simultaneously handing $24B back to shareholders over four years. I've seen management teams deleverage. I've seen them buy back stock aggressively. Doing both at this scale is unusual and it's probably the strongest argument that Kruger's capital allocation instincts are sound. AFFO still dipped 8% to $12,783M on weaker pricing, free funds flow off 6% to $6,927M, but those declines are entirely the commodity working against you.

## Business Overview

Most people bucket Suncor with Canadian Natural or Cenovus. That's a mistake, or at least it misses the structural difference. Suncor owns the whole chain, mine to gas pump. They dig up bitumen, upgrade it, refine it, and sell it to consumers at Petro-Canada stations. When oil prices got hammered in Q4 2025, the upstream side bled margin but the refining side actually picked up the slack. It's not a perfect offset, never is, but it dulls the impact in a way that a pure-play producer just can't match unless they want to go spend $15-20 billion building refineries from scratch.

**Oil Sands** is where essentially all the value sits. Bitumen mining at Base Plant, in-situ production at Firebag, plus the Syncrude and Fort Hills stakes. FY2025 pulled 860,000 bbls/d, Q4 alone was 909,000. Upgrader utilization ran at 99% for the year which is frankly absurd for assets of that complexity.

**E&P** is the offshore and conventional stuff. About 7% of upstream production. Management clearly doesn't care much about it relative to the oil sands portfolio and neither should you.

**Refining and Marketing** is really the insurance policy. Four refineries across Canada, plus Petro-Canada retail, pushing 480,000 bbls/d of throughput at 103% utilization. You don't own this segment for the standalone returns. You own it because when crude craters, refining margins usually widen, and that buys Suncor time that its peers don't get.

If you made me pick the single best thing Kruger has accomplished, it's probably the balance sheet. Net debt went from $13,639M down to $6,337M in three years, a 54% cut, while Suncor was writing $24B in checks to shareholders through dividends and buybacks at the same time. That combination just doesn't happen often. Most CEOs in energy either fix the balance sheet or they reward shareholders. ### Unit Economics

The per-barrel cost picture is where you can really see what's changed under Kruger. I backed into the numbers from OS&G in the filings: implied cost went from around $50.66/bbl in Q4 2022 ($3,556M divided by 763,100 bbls/d) to roughly $40.87/bbl by Q3 2025 ($3,270M divided by 870,000 bbls/d). That's 19% lower on a per-unit basis while producing 14% more volume. Textbook fixed-cost leverage. I'd caveat that proper segmented cost disclosures would give tighter numbers than what I'm backing into from aggregates, but the direction isn't ambiguous. And look, running above 100% utilization at both upgraders and refineries for two years straight is not normal for this industry. Most operators I follow are happy to sustain mid-90s without a major incident.

You can't really understand where Suncor is today without knowing how Kruger got the job. Elliott Management built up something like a $3.4B position and basically forced the board's hand after a string of safety incidents and operational missteps that had gotten embarrassing for a company this large. Kruger was running Imperial Oil until 2019, then retired, then got pulled back in. Since April 2023 the operating results have been, honestly, hard to argue with. Annual production highs every year since he showed up, refinery utilization above 100% two years running, net debt chopped in half. Elliott looks brilliant in retrospect. The open question, and it's a big one, is whether these improvements are structural enough to outlast the person driving them.

### Compensation and Alignment

Total comp numbers can be misleading so I tend to focus on value at risk instead. Kruger has $43,930,170 tied to the stock, which comes out to 32.5x his base salary. The guideline is 6x. So when the stock drops 10% he's personally losing about $4.4M. That's the kind of exposure where you know he's paying attention to the share price. His total comp for 2025 was $15,453,062 per the Proxy Circular, which is rich in absolute terms but not out of line for a company this size with these results.

Troy Little at CFO is a different situation. He's at $540,848 in ownership, 1.1x salary, below the 2x target. He just got the job in November 2025 so the low number has an obvious explanation, but I'd still want to see open-market purchases from him in the next couple of quarters. When a new CFO buys stock with their own money early in the tenure it signals something. When they don't, that signals something too.

### Board of Directors

The governance setup is clean on paper. Eleven board members, ten independent, Russ Girling as chair with 97% approval at the 2025 AGM. Nothing to pick at there.

Where it gets less comfortable is the actual skin in the game. Directors and officers as a group hold 268,801 common shares. On a 1.2 billion share base that's 0.02%, which, let's be honest, is effectively zero. Yes they have restricted stock and DSUs that move with the share price. But there's a psychological difference between owning shares you were granted as part of your pay package and owning shares you went out and bought with money you could have spent on something else. I'd put more weight on the latter and I don't see enough of it.

### Auditor

KPMG has served as Suncor's external auditor since 2019, with total audit fees of $7.3M.

### Succession Risk

I'll say it plainly: this is the one thing about Suncor that genuinely makes me nervous. Kruger is around 70 years old and he came out of retirement for this job. There's no public successor, the management bench is thin because the whole senior team turned over after Elliott pushed for changes, and the operating improvements are really Kruger's operating improvements. Will the culture he built persist after he's gone? I genuinely don't know. The CFO handoff in November 2025 went fine, which at least tells me the board knows how to execute a leadership transition without causing a mess. But replacing a CFO and replacing the CEO who drove a multi-year turnaround are two completely different things. If the board hasn't identified an internal candidate within the next year, that becomes a serious red flag for me.

## Guidance Track Record

A 6-point miss to the upside on a metric that management should be able to pin within a couple of points. Probably some of both. Production did the same thing: 810,000 to 840,000 guided, 860,000 actual, clearing the top end by 20,000 barrels a day. At a certain point this stops being conservatism and starts being a strategy. And it's working. By now the market should be tacking an extra 2-3% onto every operational target this team puts out there.

## Conclusion

After going through all of this, here's where I land. Suncor is a well-run company, probably the best-operated integrated in Canada right now, that has the bad luck of being a commodity business in a period where commodity prices are working against it. The normalized FFFlow growth of 15% at constant US$75 WTI is the true scorecard on management execution. The 29% AFFO decline from 2022's peak is the commodity tape. Separating those two things is basically the entire analytical exercise on this stock, and a lot of investors don't bother to do it.

Capital returns are well funded for now. $5,800M went out the door in 2025, buybacks alone are scaling to about $3,300M for 2026. At the year-end TSX price of $60.92 you're looking at a 3.9% dividend yield, which isn't going to excite anyone, but the buyback is knocking out roughly 5% of the float per year and that's where the per-share accretion actually comes from.

My worry list, ranked: Kruger's age and no named successor at the top, because everything good about this company traces back to one person and he won't be here forever. Then the $5.3B gap between AFFO at 2022 commodity prices versus 2025, which quantifies exactly how much of your return depends on oil. Then the utilization rates. I want to believe 103% is the new baseline but I'd really need to see the turnaround schedules and the deferred maintenance log before I'd commit to that view. Three years of strong performance is enough to see a trend. It's not enough to rule out mean reversion.

**HOLD.** Good enough to own. Not cheap enough to buy more. If oil recovers, or Kruger names a successor, or I see maintenance schedules that make me comfortable with the utilization trajectory, I'd reconsider. Until then I'm sitting.

## Bullish Investment Thesis

*Updated March 11, 2026. Incorporates current commodity pricing (WTI ~US$87, Brent ~US$92), the 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis, Suncor's FY2026 corporate guidance, TMX pipeline data, and the Carney government's energy policy pivot. External market data below was not found in Suncor filings; sources are listed in the External Market Data section at the end of this report.*

Start with the valuation because that's what jumped out at me first. Suncor at 5.8x EV/EBITDA when Canadian Natural is at 7.3x. Suncor at 9.5x P/FCF when Cenovus is at 16.7x. These are March 9, 2026 numbers at C$77.50. I can't square that discount with the operating metrics. Suncor is outperforming both of them on production growth, utilization, and cost per barrel. At roughly US$87 WTI against a breakeven in the low $40s, every barrel coming out of the ground generates about US$45 of margin before royalties. That's enough to fund the entire capital return program and then some. The highest analyst target out there is C$88.48, and that was set before the Hormuz situation pushed crude way above where anyone expected it to be this year.

About Hormuz. I'll be upfront, I've watched probably five or six "the strait is closing" scares since I started paying attention to energy markets and every single one fizzled. This one hasn't. It's been shut since March 2 and 17 to 20 million barrels per day of transit capacity is genuinely offline. Brent touched US$119 before the IEA released 400 million barrels from strategic reserves and pulled it back to around US$92. Canada, almost overnight, became what the Globe and Mail called "the market's most reliable oil supplier." Suncor fits that thesis perfectly, landlocked North American production, CUSMA-compliant, zero tariff risk. TMX has taken the WCS-WTI spread from around US$18.65 in 2023 to an estimated US$12.00 for 2026, and Suncor is locked into TMX capacity under a 15 to 20 year take-or-pay deal. On top of all that, US shale output is actually declining for the first time (EIA projects 13.5 million bbls/d for 2026, off 100,000 from 2025) and OPEC+ only added 206,000 bpd for April. The supply side is tight and there's no obvious source of incremental barrels to loosen it in the near term.

Even without the geopolitical tailwind, the operational story holds up on its own. FY2026 guidance has production at 840,000 to 870,000 bbls/d on lower capex of C$5.6 to C$5.8B versus C$6.1 to C$6.3B in 2025. More output, less spending, which is exactly what you want to see from a maturing oil sands operation. And knowing how Kruger guides, the actuals will probably come in above the top end again. The NCIB renewal on March 3 authorized buybacks of up to 118.7 million shares, roughly 10% of the float, through March 2027. Between that and the $0.60 quarterly dividend you're looking at a total shareholder yield around 9 to 10% at the current price.

One thing that caught my attention in the filings that I don't think gets talked about enough: Fisher Asset Management increased their position by 85.5% to 15.5 million shares. That's not index rebalancing. Somebody at Fisher looked at this name and made a deliberate call to increase by almost double. On the policy front, the picture has shifted in ways that would have seemed unlikely even a year ago. Carney's government dropped the consumer carbon tax, Alberta froze industrial carbon pricing at $95 per tonne, and there's now a Canada-Alberta MOU for a northwest coast pipeline that creates an egress option the industry didn't have before. The political overhang on Canadian oil sands is lighter than it's been in a decade.

March 31 Investor Day in Toronto should be the next thing to watch. Kruger will almost certainly guide conservatively on medium-term targets and almost certainly beat whatever he puts out. At current prices Suncor is generating enough free cash to retire 10% of the float every year while carrying 0.50x net debt to AFFO and growing the dividend. If Hormuz persists or escalates, Brent goes back above US$100 (Goldman's scenario) and AFFO would blow through the FY2022 peak of $18.1B. If the crisis resolves tomorrow, pre-crisis consensus was US$64 to US$70 Brent which still leaves Suncor well above breakeven. I have a hard time building a model where this stock doesn't work from here unless oil goes below $50 and stays there for an extended period. And if that's your view, the problem isn't Suncor specifically, it's the entire Canadian energy sector.

## Bearish Investment Thesis

*Updated March 11, 2026. Incorporates current commodity pricing (WTI ~US$87, Brent ~US$92), the 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis, global inventory data, recession risk indicators, and Canadian regulatory developments. External market data below was not found in Suncor filings; sources are listed in the External Market Data section at the end of this report.*

The thing that nags at me about buying Suncor here is the price you're paying for it. C$77.50 is above where the average analyst has the stock (C$74.78). It's within shouting distance of the 52-week high at C$80.12. And the stock has roughly doubled from its low of C$43.59. That entire move happened largely because of a geopolitical crisis that, by its nature, will eventually end. Every Hormuz scare I've seen over the years has resolved. When this one does, pre-crisis consensus had Brent at US$59 to US$64 for 2026, that's from the five-bank Wall Street average compiled by Bloomberg. J.P. Morgan's downside case is even grimmer: WTI in the US$30s by 2027 under a surplus scenario. At US$60 Suncor's AFFO goes back to roughly where it was in 2025, which was already declining. At US$50, you're staring at a dividend cut. Putting fresh money into a war premium at all-time highs isn't something I'd do.

Take the geopolitics away and look at what the oil market actually looks like underneath. There are 477 million barrels of excess inventory that accumulated in 2025. That's not a small number, that's almost half a billion barrels of oil the world produced and didn't consume. The EIA has supply growing by 2.4 million bpd in 2026 while the IEA puts demand growth at only 930,000 bpd. Supply is outpacing demand by more than two to one. The Hormuz disruption is basically hiding a surplus, and we got a preview of how quickly the floor can drop out: the IEA's emergency release of 400 million barrels moved Brent from US$119 to US$92 within days. That kind of velocity tells you the underlying bid for crude at these levels is fragile.

Now layer on a recession, which J.P. Morgan gives a 35% shot for 2026. In that world, the existing inventory glut meets falling demand, and WTI probably tests the low US$50s. Suncor's free funds flow at that price would be a fraction of the $6,927M from FY2025. The buyback program, which is running at $3,300M annually and is arguably the centerpiece of the bull case, becomes unaffordable. Breakeven in the low $40s keeps the company solvent, sure. It doesn't keep the share price anywhere near C$77.

The regulatory environment is messier than the bulls want to acknowledge. The federal emissions cap requires registration starting 2026 with compliance kicking in by 2030, targeting a 35% cut from 2019 levels. Alberta's government froze industrial carbon pricing at $95/tonne, but the federal schedule marches up to $170/tonne by 2030 and Ottawa has given no indication of backing down. Those two positions are flatly contradictory and at some point the standoff resolves, probably not in the industry's favor. The Pathways Alliance CCS project was supposed to have a final investment decision by end-2025. It didn't. The consortium apparently wants over C$12B in public subsidies before they'll break ground. I'm not optimistic that money materializes quickly, and every year without CCS makes the per-barrel compliance cost math worse.

I've already covered the succession question at length so I'll keep it brief here: Kruger is 70 with no named replacement, the management bench is shallow, and insiders collectively own 0.02% of the company. Those are facts, not opinions. Add to that a consensus forecast for a stronger Canadian dollar (USD/CAD from 1.37 toward 1.33-1.35 by year-end) which compresses margins about 3% on USD revenue against CAD costs, and a climate-risk governance proposal on the May 5 AGM ballot that could mark the beginning of sustained shareholder pressure on decarbonization spending.

I'm not building a new position at these levels. The stock is trading above consensus, near the high, into a geopolitical premium with half a billion barrels of surplus inventory sitting underneath it. Take away Hormuz and WTI settles in the low US$60s, which is fine for keeping the operation running but doesn't justify the current valuation. Emissions cap uncertainty, CCS delays, succession risk, and a strengthening loonie are headwinds that nobody seems to be pricing while the war dominates the headlines. I'd want to see either C$65 or lower on a commodity pullback, or resolution of the structural overhangs (named CEO successor, emissions cap finalized, Pathways Alliance commitment) before I'd put new capital to work. If crude reverts to US$55-60 post-crisis and compliance costs keep rising, this stock has a lot further to fall than the market currently appreciates.
