# Suncor Energy (TSX: SU) | Equity Research Report
> Recommendation: HOLD | Data through Q4 2025 | All figures in C$ unless noted | Sources: Suncor earnings releases and SEC filings only

## Investment Thesis

**HOLD.** The tension at the heart of this name is simple: Suncor is operationally the best it's ever been, and the financial results are getting worse anyway. Normalized free funds flow at a constant US$75 WTI jumped 15% to $8,476M in 2025, which is the number that matters most because it strips out the one variable management can't control. Production, utilization, cost per barrel: all moving in the right direction, for three years running now. I've rarely seen a Canadian oil sands operator sustain this kind of execution streak.

But AFFO dropped from $18,101M (2022) to $12,783M (2025), and free funds flow fell from $13,114M to $6,927M over the same stretch. That's all commodity prices. And here's the problem: Suncor doesn't publish oil price forecasts or hedging positions, so from filings alone, I can't model where this goes. At the December 31, 2025 TSX close of $60.92 (per the 2026 Proxy Circular), you're getting a growing dividend ($0.60/quarter), accelerating buybacks ($275M/month in 2026), and what I'd argue is the best-run integrated oil business in North America. That's enough to hold. Not enough to add.

## Executive Summary

Forget the headline financials for a moment. The number to focus on is $8,476M. That's Suncor's normalized free funds flow at a constant US$75 WTI, up 15% from $7,373M a year earlier. At the same oil price, Suncor generated $1.1 billion more cash than it did twelve months ago. That tells you the operational improvement is real, not just a commodity tailwind wearing a disguise.

The operating details are in the snapshot table below. What stands out isn't any single metric; it's that production (860,000 bbls/d), refinery utilization (103%), and throughput (480,000 bbls/d) all hit new highs simultaneously. I'd want to understand how sustainable 103% utilization really is. Running above nameplate for two consecutive years is unusual anywhere in the industry, and the Q2 2025 upgrader outage showed how quickly one disruption can crater a quarter.

Meanwhile, net debt plunged 54% from $13,639M to $6,337M over three years while the company returned roughly $24B to shareholders. That combination, deleveraging and paying out at the same time, is what separates this management team from its predecessors. The financial drag is entirely commodity prices: AFFO dipped 8% to $12,783M and free funds flow slid 6% to $6,927M.

## Business Overview

What makes Suncor different from a Canadian Natural or a Cenovus is the integration. This isn't just an oil sands producer; it's a mine-to-pump operation that captures margin at every stage: extraction, upgrading, refining, and Petro-Canada retail. When crude prices collapsed in Q4 2025, upstream margins shrank but refining margins widened. The downstream absorbed part of the blow. That hedge doesn't make Suncor immune to commodity cycles, but it smooths the ride in a way pure upstream players can't replicate without spending tens of billions they don't have.

**Oil Sands** is 93% of the story. Bitumen mining (Base Plant), in-situ extraction (Firebag), and the Syncrude and Fort Hills interests. FY2025 upstream production: 860,000 bbls/d, with Q4 at 909,000. Upgrader utilization: 99% for the full year.

**E&P** is offshore and conventional, roughly 7% of upstream production. Not where the capital or management attention is going.

**Refining and Marketing** is the hedge. Four Canadian refineries, Petro-Canada retail, 480,000 bbls/d of throughput at 103% utilization. The segment's value isn't its standalone profitability; it's the counter-cyclical cushion it provides when upstream pricing deteriorates.

The balance sheet transformation is the single most impressive thing Kruger has done. Net debt plunged 54% from $13,639M to $6,337M over 2022 to 2025 while the company simultaneously returned roughly $24B to shareholders. Most management teams pick one or the other: deleverage or return capital. Kruger did both, and the $8B net debt target was achieved nine months ahead of schedule. At YE 2025, net debt to AFFO sits at 0.50x. For context, that's about half the leverage I'd consider comfortable for an integrated oil company. Total debt edged down from $10,345M to $9,987M over 2024 to 2025, though I'd note the gross number is still $10B. The low net debt figure partly reflects a cash position that can evaporate quickly through buybacks and working capital swings.

Management's stated policy is 100% of excess free funds flow to shareholders. So far, they've delivered on that. ### Unit Economics

Here's where you can see the turnaround in a single line. Implied per-barrel OS&G dropped from roughly $50.66/bbl (Q4 2022) to roughly $40.87/bbl (Q3 2025) , a 19% decline while production grew 14%. That's the definition of operating leverage: more barrels through the same fixed-cost base. The math comes from OS&G data in the filings ($3,556M / 763,100 bbls/d vs. $3,270M / 870,000 bbls/d), and I'd caveat that segmented cost disclosures would give a more precise picture. But the direction is unambiguous. Running upgraders and refineries above 100% utilization isn't something most operators manage for a quarter, let alone two consecutive years.

Kruger's backstory matters for understanding this company. Elliott Management took a roughly $3.4B stake and forced a leadership change after a series of safety incidents and operational misses that had become embarrassing for a company of Suncor's stature. Kruger came out of retirement (he'd been running Imperial Oil until 2019), and the results since April 2023 have been the kind of turnaround that makes activist investors look like geniuses. Production highs every year, 100%+ refinery utilization in both 2024 and 2025, net debt cut in half. The question isn't whether he delivered. It's what happens when he leaves.

### Compensation and Alignment

I always look at value at risk, not total comp, for alignment. Kruger's is $43,930,170, or 32.5 times his base salary, well above the 6x guideline. His total comp for 2025 was $15,453,062 (per the 2026 Proxy Circular). The ratio means a 10% move in SU stock swings his personal wealth by roughly $4.4M. That's real alignment.

CFO Troy Little is the weak spot. Ownership at $540,848 (1.1x salary) against a 2x guideline. He was only appointed in November 2025, so the low number is understandable, but I'd want to see him buying shares out of pocket over the next two quarters as a credibility signal.

### Board of Directors

Board governance checks the right boxes: 11 members, 10 independent, 97% approval for Chair Russ Girling at the 2025 AGM. No red flags there.

Insider ownership is a different story. Directors and officers collectively own 268,801 common shares, just 0.02% of shares outstanding. That's essentially nothing. The restricted stock and DSU structure ties compensation to the stock price, which helps, but I'd be more comfortable if I saw executives buying shares on the open market rather than just holding what they were granted.

### Auditor

KPMG has served as Suncor's external auditor since 2019, with total audit fees of $7.3M.

### Succession Risk

This is the risk that keeps me up at night on this name. Kruger is roughly 70. He was recruited from retirement. No successor has been publicly named, and average management tenure is short because most of the senior team was brought in during the post-Elliott refresh. The operational turnaround is Kruger's turnaround. It's not yet clear whether the culture changes survive him. The CFO transition in November 2025 was orderly, which is a good sign for how the board handles leadership changes, but the CEO succession is a different magnitude of problem. I'd want to see an internal candidate named within the next 12 months.

When Kruger's team gives 2026 guidance, the market should be adding 2-3% to every operational target.

## Conclusion

I've spent a lot of time in these filings, and the conclusion I keep arriving at is that Suncor is a genuinely well-run company trapped in a commodity price environment that makes it look mediocre on paper. The normalized FFFlow growth (+15% at constant US$75 WTI) is the real performance. The headline AFFO decline (down 29% from 2022's peak) is the commodity environment. Distinguishing between the two is the whole analytical challenge of this name.

The capital return program ($5,800M in 2025, climbing to roughly $3,300M in buybacks alone for 2026) is well-funded at current prices. At the December 31, 2025 TSX price of $60.92, the quarterly dividend implies a yield of roughly 3.9%. That's not exciting, but the buyback (retiring roughly 5% of the float annually) is where the real per-share accretion comes from.

What worries me, in order: first, Kruger's age and the absence of a named successor. This operational culture hasn't been tested without him yet. Second, the $5.3B annual gap between AFFO at 2022 commodity prices and 2025 prices, which tells you exactly how much this stock is a bet on oil. Third, the sustainability of 103% utilization. I'd want to see the asset maintenance schedules before assuming this is the new normal rather than a multi-year best that reverts.

**HOLD.** The execution justifies owning it. The commodity uncertainty, which Suncor itself can't forecast and doesn't try to, means I can't justify buying more. Reassess on: a sustained commodity recovery, CEO succession clarity, or the first quarter where operational metrics visibly deteriorate.

## Bullish Investment Thesis

*Updated March 11, 2026. Incorporates current commodity pricing (WTI ~US$87, Brent ~US$92), the 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis, Suncor's FY2026 corporate guidance, TMX pipeline data, and the Carney government's energy policy pivot. External market data below was not found in Suncor filings; sources are listed in the External Market Data section at the end of this report.*

The bull case starts with a valuation gap that shouldn't exist. Suncor trades at 5.8x EV/EBITDA versus 7.3x for Canadian Natural and 9.5x P/FCF versus 16.7x for Cenovus (as of March 9, 2026 at C$77.50). That's a steep discount for a company that's outperforming both on operational metrics. With WTI at roughly US$87 against a low-$40s breakeven, every barrel Suncor produces generates roughly US$45 of margin before royalties. The kind of cushion that funds the entire capital return program with room to spare. If commodity prices hold anywhere near current levels, the AFFO decline that defined 2022-2025 reverses. The highest analyst target of C$88.48 implies 14% upside, and it was set before Hormuz pushed crude well above the pre-crisis consensus of US$59 to US$64 Brent.

Then there's Hormuz. I'm old enough to remember every "this time the strait closes for real" scare over the past two decades, and none of them stuck. This one is different. The closure since March 2, 2026 has actually removed 17 to 20 million barrels per day of transit capacity, Brent spiked to US$119 before the IEA's 400 million barrel reserve release brought it back to roughly US$92. Canada suddenly looks like the last reliable oil supplier standing (the Globe and Mail's phrase, not mine). Suncor's production is entirely landlocked North American supply, 100% CUSMA-compliant, zero tariff exposure. The TMX expansion has structurally narrowed the WCS-WTI differential from roughly US$18.65 (2023) to roughly US$12.00 (2026 forecast), and Suncor is locked in under a 15 to 20 year take-or-pay contract. Add US shale declining for the first time in 2026 (EIA: 13.5 million bbls/d, down 100,000 from 2025) and OPEC+ adding only a marginal 206,000 bpd for April, and the supply picture looks tight enough to sustain a premium for quarters, not just weeks.

Even if the geopolitical premium fades tomorrow, the operational trajectory supports the stock. FY2026 guidance targets 840,000 to 870,000 bbls/d on lower capex of C$5.6 to C$5.8B (down from C$6.1 to C$6.3B in 2025). More barrels, less capital. And given Kruger's track record of sandbagging guidance, the actual numbers will likely be higher. The NCIB renewal (March 3, 2026) authorizes repurchase of up to 118.7 million shares, roughly 10% of the public float. Combined with the $0.60 quarterly dividend, total shareholder yield is running at 9 to 10%.

What I find most telling is the institutional money flow. Fisher Asset Management grew its position by 85.5% to 15.5 million shares. That's not a passive index rebalance; that's a conviction bet. Meanwhile, the policy environment has flipped: the Carney government eliminated the consumer carbon tax, Alberta froze industrial carbon pricing at $95 per tonne, and the Canada-Alberta MOU for a northwest coast oil pipeline creates an egress option that didn't exist a year ago. The political risk that weighed on Canadian oil sands for the past decade is lighter than it's been since 2015.

The March 31 Investor Day in Toronto is the next catalyst. If Kruger follows his established playbook, the medium-term targets will be conservative, and the market should price in a beat. At current commodity prices, Suncor is generating free cash flow at a pace that could retire 10% of its float annually while maintaining a growing dividend at 0.50x net debt to AFFO. The asymmetry is compelling: if Hormuz extends, Brent revisits US$100+ (Goldman Sachs scenario) and AFFO blows past FY2022's $18.1B peak. If the crisis fades, the pre-crisis base case of US$64 to US$70 Brent still leaves Suncor operating well above its low-$40s breakeven. I struggle to construct a scenario where the stock doesn't work at a discount to peers with this operational profile, unless you believe oil is going below $50 and staying there. And if you believe that, you shouldn't own any Canadian energy name, not just this one.

## Bearish Investment Thesis

*Updated March 11, 2026. Incorporates current commodity pricing (WTI ~US$87, Brent ~US$92), the 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis, global inventory data, recession risk indicators, and Canadian regulatory developments. External market data below was not found in Suncor filings; sources are listed in the External Market Data section at the end of this report.*

Here's what bothers me about buying Suncor at C$77.50: the stock is above the average analyst target (C$74.78), within 2% of its 52-week high (C$80.12), and has nearly doubled from the low (C$43.59). You're paying full freight for a geopolitical event (the Hormuz closure) that is by definition temporary. Every previous strait disruption scare in my career has resolved, and when this one does, pre-conflict consensus pointed to Brent at US$59 to US$64 for 2026 (Wall Street five-bank average per Bloomberg). J.P. Morgan's bear case goes further: US$30s WTI in 2027 under a surplus scenario. At US$60 WTI, Suncor's AFFO compresses back toward FY2025's already-declining $12,783M. At US$50, the math breaks. Buying at all-time highs into a war premium is not a risk I'd take with new capital.

Strip away Hormuz, and the fundamentals are ugly. Global observed oil inventories rose by 477 million barrels in 2025. That's not a rounding error, that's nearly half a billion barrels of surplus the market is sitting on. The EIA projects further builds of 1.9 to 2.1 million barrels per day in 2026. Supply growth (2.4 million bpd) outpaces demand growth (930,000 bpd per IEA) by more than 2:1. The Hormuz disruption is papering over a structural surplus, and we already saw how fast the premium can evaporate: the IEA's 400 million barrel reserve release took Brent from US$119 to US$92 in days, not weeks.

Add a recession (J.P. Morgan puts the probability at 35% for 2026) and the inventory overhang accelerates into demand destruction. At US$55 WTI, Suncor's free funds flow shrinks to a fraction of the $6,927M reported for FY2025, and the $3,300M annualized buyback program, the core of the bull thesis, becomes mathematically unsustainable. The low-$40s breakeven keeps the lights on, but it doesn't protect the stock price.

The regulatory picture is murkier than the bull case admits. Canada's federal emissions cap (registration 2026, compliance 2030) targets a 35% reduction from 2019 levels. Alberta froze industrial carbon pricing at $95/tonne, but the federal schedule goes to $170/tonne by 2030, and Ottawa hasn't blinked. Someone is going to lose that fight. The Pathways Alliance CCS hub, the industry's answer to the cap, has stalled without a final investment decision despite promising one by end-2025. They're reportedly holding out for over C$12B in public subsidies. I'm skeptical that gets resolved quickly, and without CCS, the compliance cost math gets worse every year.

On succession: I've said it elsewhere in this report, but it bears repeating here in the bear context. Kruger is roughly 70 with no named successor. Insider ownership is 268,801 shares (0.02% of outstanding), which is negligible. A stronger Canadian dollar (consensus: USD/CAD moving from 1.37 to 1.33-1.35 by year-end) squeezes margins by roughly 3% on USD revenues against CAD costs. And the May 5 AGM includes a shareholder proposal on climate-risk governance, potentially the first shot in what becomes a sustained activist campaign on decarbonization spending.

If I'm building a position from scratch, I'm not paying C$77.50. Not above the consensus target, not within 2% of the high, not into a war premium with 477 million barrels of surplus inventory sitting underneath it. Without Hormuz, the base case points to WTI in the low US$60s. Enough for Suncor to operate comfortably, not enough to justify the current multiple. The emissions cap, CCS delays, succession uncertainty, and CAD strengthening are all headwinds the market isn't pricing because it's distracted by geopolitics. My approach: wait for either a commodity correction that offers a better entry below C$65, or wait for resolution of the structural risks (CEO succession, emissions cap finalization, Pathways Alliance FID) before committing new capital. The downside scenario (post-crisis WTI reversion to US$55 to US$60 combined with rising compliance costs) points to a stock price well below C$60, and I don't think the market is discounting that possibility at all right now.
